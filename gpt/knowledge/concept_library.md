## [02] تجربة كافندش — وزن الأرض
_The Cavendish Experiment — weighing the Earth_
الفصل 1 · الدرس 1-1 · slug: `cavendish` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Newton gave us the law. Every mass pulls every other mass.
> But the law has a number in it. A constant. Big G. And nobody knew what it was.
> For a hundred years, G was a question mark. The Earth's pull, we could feel. The constant behind it, we could not.
> The problem: to measure G, you need to watch two ordinary objects pull each other. Not a planet. Just two weights.
> And that pull is absurdly weak. Two lead spheres a foot apart attract each other with less force than the weight of a single grain of sand.
> Cavendish's idea was patient. Hang two small masses from a thin fiber. Like a tiny barbell on a thread.
> Bring two large masses close. The small ones drift toward them. Just barely.
> The fiber twists. And how much it twists tells you exactly how hard they pulled.
> From a twist you can barely see, Cavendish pulled out G.
> And once you have G, the law works backward. The same equation that drops an apple can now weigh the entire planet.
> He didn't visit space. He didn't lift the Earth. He measured a thread, in a room, and held a planet on a number.
> The fiber, the masses, the deflection — that's the apparatus. Extracting the value of G from the measured twist, then using it to compute the Earth's mass — that's yours.

## [03] مجال الجاذبية
_The Gravitational Field_
الفصل 1 · الدرس 1-2 · slug: `gfield` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Drop something. It falls. We say the Earth pulls it.
> But here's a strange question. How does the rock know the Earth is there? They never touch.
> Newton himself was uneasy about this. Action at a distance, across empty space, with nothing in between.
> So we change the picture. Instead of the Earth reaching out to grab things —
> — the Earth changes the space around it. Everywhere. Whether anything is there to feel it or not.
> That invisible influence has a name. The gravitational field. A property of the space, not of the rock.
> The rock doesn't reach out to the Earth. It just responds to the field it's sitting in. Right here. Locally.
> Closer to the Earth, the arrows crowd together. The field is stronger.
> Far away, they thin out. Never quite zero. Just fainter, and fainter.
> Weight isn't a thing an object owns. It's the field, times the mass, measured where the object happens to be.
> Same rock. Different field. Different weight. Move it to the Moon and the arrows are weaker — the rock didn't change.
> The field is the map of the influence. Computing the field strength at a given distance from a mass, and the force it puts on an object placed there — that's yours.

## [04] انعدام الوزن والسقوط الحر
_Weightlessness & Free Fall (orbit as perpetual falling)_

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Astronauts float. The easy explanation: there's no gravity up there.
> That explanation is wrong. At the space station's height, Earth's gravity is almost as strong as it is on the ground.
> So if gravity is still pulling them — why don't they feel it?
> Go back to Earth. An elevator. You stand on a scale. It reads your weight.
> Now cut the cable. Elevator, scale, and you — all falling together.
> The scale reads zero. Not because gravity stopped. Because the floor is falling away exactly as fast as you are.
> That's weightlessness. Not the absence of gravity. The absence of anything pushing back.
> Now the trick. Throw a ball. It curves down and hits the ground.
> Throw it harder. It lands farther. Harder still — farther still.
> Throw it fast enough—about eight kilometers per second—and the ground curves away beneath it just as fast as it falls. It keeps falling, and never lands.
> That's an orbit. The station isn't escaping gravity. It's falling around the Earth, forever, missing the ground.
> Falling, with nothing to push back. How fast must you go to keep missing the ground? Set falling equal to curving — and solve.

## [05] كتلة القصور وكتلة الجاذبية ومبدأ التكافؤ
_Inertial vs Gravitational Mass & the Equivalence Principle_
الفصل 1 · الدرس 1-2 · slug: `equivalence` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> The word mass hides two completely different ideas.
> One is stubbornness. Push a thing; how hard is it to get moving? That's inertial mass.
> The other is heaviness. How strongly does gravity pull on it? That's gravitational mass.
> There's no reason these should be the same number. Resistance to a push, and the strength of a pull — different questions.
> And yet. Galileo's old observation: drop a heavy thing and a light thing together —
> — they land at the same instant. Always. Every time anyone has ever checked.
> That only works if the stubbornness and the heaviness cancel perfectly. The two masses are equal.
> A coincidence, said Newton. A fact you write down and use.
> Not a coincidence, said Einstein. A clue.
> His thought: stand in a sealed box. The floor presses your feet. Are you on Earth — or in a rocket accelerating through empty space?
> From inside, you cannot tell. Gravity and acceleration feel exactly the same. That's the equivalence principle.
> Two views of the same equal masses — one calls it luck, one calls it the door to a new theory. Which experiments would tell them apart? That's the thinking we leave with you.

> But in another sense, they move together. Same start, same finish, same instant. They never separate.

> So the child at the edge really is faster — and yet they turn at the very same rate. No contradiction.

## [07] العزم وذراع القوة

> A stuck bolt. You push the wrench with everything you have. Nothing.
> Then you slide a pipe over the handle to make it longer. Push gently. It turns.
> Less force. More effect. Something other than force is doing the work here.
> Push right at the bolt's center — and nothing turns, no matter how hard.

> That perpendicular reach has a name. The lever arm. Lengthen it, and the same force turns harder.
> A door proves it daily. Push at the handle, it swings. Push next to the hinge, it fights you.
> When several torques act, the rotation goes to whichever side wins the sum.
> Computing the torque from a given force and distance — and finding equilibrium when multiple torques act — that's yours.

## [08] مركز الكتلة والاتزان

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Throw a wrench, spinning, across the room.
> Every part of it goes a different way. But one single point traces a clean, smooth arc.
> That point moves as if all the mass were squeezed into it. The center of mass.
> It isn't always in the middle of the object. It isn't even always inside the object.
> Now stand a block on the table. Tilt it a little. It rocks back down.
> Tilt it more. There's a moment where it hangs, undecided.
> Past that moment, it goes over. What flipped wasn't the push. It was whether the center of mass stayed above the base.
> That's the whole rule of stability. Center of mass over the base of support — it returns. Beyond it — it falls.
> This is why a low, wide thing is hard to tip. A race car. A pyramid.
> And why a person leans into a heavy bag — moving their center of mass back over their feet.
> Lower the center, widen the base, keep it over support — that's balance, with no magic in it.
> Locating the center of mass of a given shape, and finding the maximum tilt before its vertical line leaves the base — that's yours.

## [09] القوى الظاهرية: الطاردة المركزية وكوريوليس

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> A car takes a sharp turn. You feel thrown against the door.
> It feels like a force pushed you outward. Name it, and it seems real.
> But step outside the car. Watch from above, from the still ground.
> From out here, nothing pushed you outward. Your body simply wanted to go straight — and the car turned out from under you.
> The outward push was never a force. It was the feeling of your own straight-line motion, seen from a turning frame.
> That invented push has a name we use anyway, because it's useful inside the turning frame. The centrifugal force.
> Now a harder one. Stand at the center of a spinning platform and roll a ball straight to a friend at the edge.
> From above, it goes perfectly straight. From on the platform, it curves away — and misses.
> Nothing bent the ball's path through space—it went perfectly straight. But the floor rotated beneath that straight path, so from the floor it looks curved.
> That apparent deflection has a name. The Coriolis effect — the signature of doing physics on a turning floor.
> We live on one. The Earth is the spinning platform. It steers winds and ocean currents into great curves.
> These forces vanish the moment you step to the still ground. When do you need them, and how big are they? That's the thinking to carry off.

## [10] نظرية الدفع والزخم
_Impulse–Momentum Theorem_
الفصل 3 · الدرس 3-1 · slug: `impulse` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Two cars hit a wall at the same speed. One crumples slowly. One stops instantly.
> Same speed in. Same dead stop. One driver walks away. One does not.
> The motion that had to be killed was identical. So what made the difference?
> Call that quantity of motion momentum — mass moving, how much there is to stop.
> To change momentum you need a force. But force alone isn't the whole story.
> A force acting for a long time, and a much bigger force acting for an instant, can do the very same job.
> It's force multiplied by the time it acts. That product is the impulse.
> And the impulse equals exactly the change in momentum. That's the whole bridge.
> Now the crash makes sense. Same momentum to remove. The crumple zone stretches the stopping time —
> — so the force needed drops. The rigid car stops in an instant, and the force spikes.
> Airbags, knees bending on landing, a boxer rolling with a punch—all the same trick. Buy time, cut the force. Watch: the airbag stretches the stop from a tenth of a second to half a second—force drops fivefold.
> Computing the impulse from a force acting over a time interval, and the change in momentum it produces — that's yours.

## [11] حفظ الزخم
_Conservation of Momentum_
الفصل 3 · الدرس 3-2 · slug: `momentumcons` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Stand on a frozen lake, perfectly still. Throw a heavy bag forward.
> You slide backward. You didn't push the ground. The bag did nothing to the world but leave your hands.
> Before: nothing moving. After: two things moving, opposite ways. Where did that motion come from?
> Add it up the right way — motion as momentum, with direction. Forward counts as plus. Backward as minus.
> The bag's forward momentum and your backward momentum are equal and opposite. They cancel.
> Total momentum before: zero. Total after: still zero. Nothing was created. It only got shared.
> This holds whenever no outside force interferes. A system left to itself. Isolated.
> And it's just Newton's third law, looked at over time. Every push has an equal, opposite push back.
> It's how a rocket climbs. Throw exhaust down hard; the rocket is thrown up just as hard.
> And how collisions work. Two carts meet. Whatever one gains, the other gives up. The total never changes.
> Inside the boundary, momentum is currency. It moves around. It is never spent.
> Setting total momentum before equal to total momentum after, and solving for an unknown velocity in a collision or recoil — that's yours.

## [12] نظرية الشغل والطاقة
_Work–Energy Theorem_
الفصل 4 · الدرس 4-1 · slug: `workenergy` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Push hard against a wall for an hour. You're exhausted. The wall hasn't moved.
> You're exhausted—your muscles burned energy. But in physics, you did no work on the wall. None.
> Work isn't effort. Work isn't being tired. Work is a force that actually moves something along its direction.
> Now push a cart and let it roll. Same force — but this time it moves.
> Force, times the distance it pushes through. That's work. And it isn't lost — it goes somewhere.
> Where it goes: into motion. The cart that was still is now fast.
> That motion is itself a kind of energy — energy of movement. The work became speed.
> Do negative work — push backward, like a brake — and you take that energy out. The cart slows.
> So work is the accounting of energy moving in and out of motion. Add it, motion grows. Remove it, motion shrinks.
> That's the work–energy theorem. The net work done on a thing equals the change in its energy of motion. Exactly.
> It's why the wall taught you nothing. No motion, no work, no change. Effort isn't in the equation.
> Computing the net work on an object and equating it to the change in its kinetic energy to find a final speed — that's yours.

## [14] القدرة
_Power — the rate of doing work_
الفصل 4 · الدرس 4-1 · slug: `power` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Carry a heavy box up four flights of stairs. Take ten minutes, resting often.
> Now carry the same box, up the same stairs, in twenty seconds.
> Here's the thing. The work done was exactly the same. Same weight. Same height.
> Same work — but the sprint nearly killed you and the stroll didn't. Work alone doesn't capture that.
> What's different isn't how much. It's how fast. The same job, packed into less time.
> Work divided by the time it took. That's power.
> Same work, less time — more power. Same work, more time — less power. It's a rate.
> It's why a small engine and a big engine can move the same car the same mile — but not in the same time.
> A weak motor can lift a piano. Eventually. A strong one lifts it now. Same work, different power.
> The unit honors the man who measured engines against horses. The watt.
> Energy is what you transfer. Power is how fast you transfer it.
> Computing power as work divided by time — and as force times speed — for a real climb or engine — that's yours.

_Collisions — Elastic & Inelastic_

> Two balls collide. We already know one rule never breaks: total momentum stays the same.
> But momentum doesn't tell the whole story of the crash. Listen to the difference.
> Two steel balls click and bounce apart, lively as before.
> Two balls of clay hit and just — stop. Stuck together. Dead.

> So a collision has two questions. Momentum: always conserved. Motion energy: only sometimes.

## [23] انتقال الحرارة: التوصيل والحمل والإشعاع
_Heat Transfer — Conduction, Convection, Radiation_
الفصل 6 · الدرس 6-1 · slug: `heattransfer` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Hold a metal spoon in hot soup. Soon the handle, far from the soup, is hot too.
> Nothing moved up that handle. So how did the heat travel through solid metal?
> The hot end's particles jiggle hard. They knock their neighbors, who knock theirs. The jiggle passes along.
> Heat passed by contact, particle to particle, the material itself staying put. That's conduction.
> Now a pot of water on a flame. The bottom heats first. But the whole pot warms — and the water churns.
> Hot water expands, grows light, rises. Cool water sinks to take its place. The fluid carries the heat by moving.
> Heat carried by the bulk motion of a fluid. That's convection.
> But step into sunlight. The Sun's heat crossed a hundred million miles of empty space. No metal. No fluid. Nothing to carry it.
> It came as light. Heat that travels as radiation needs no material at all.
> You feel it from a campfire across the gap, and from a hot stove before you ever touch it.
> Three paths for one thing. Touch. Flow. Across nothing at all.

_Specific Heat_
الفصل 6 · الدرس 6-1 · slug: `specificheat` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Same flame. Same time. Under one pot of water — and one pot of cooking oil.
> Equal heat poured into each. But the oil is scorching while the water is merely warm.
> Same energy in. Wildly different temperature out. Why does water resist heating up?
> Some substances soak up a lot of energy for only a small rise in temperature.
> Others heat fast — a little energy sends their temperature shooting up.
> That stubbornness — how much energy one gram needs to warm by one degree — is the specific heat.
> Water's is famously huge. It's one of the hardest common things to heat — and to cool.
> That's why the sea is mild while the desert sand next to it bakes by noon and freezes by night.
> It's why water cools an engine, why a hot-water bottle stays warm for hours.
> And it's why your own body, mostly water, holds a steady temperature through a changing day.
> Heat needed depends on three things: the substance, how much of it, and how big a rise you want.

## [25] الحرارة الكامنة لتغير الحالة
_Latent Heat — energy hidden in a change of state_
الفصل 6 · الدرس 6-2 · slug: `latentheat` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Heat a glass of ice water and watch the thermometer.
> You're pouring energy in steadily. But while ice remains, the temperature does not budge. Zero. Still zero.
> Energy in, no temperature rise. The heat is going somewhere we can't see on the thermometer.
> Zoom in. In ice, particles are locked in a rigid lattice. The incoming energy isn't speeding them up —
> — it's breaking the bonds that hold them in place. Energy spent on freedom, not on speed.
> Only once the last of the ice is freed does the thermometer start climbing again.
> That hidden energy — paid to change state, not to change temperature — is the latent heat.
> The same pause happens again at boiling. Energy floods in to tear liquid into vapor — temperature holds flat.
> And it runs in reverse. Steam releases that hidden energy when it condenses—which is why steam delivers far more heat to skin than boiling water at the same temperature.
> It's why sweat cools you: evaporating water carries off enormous hidden energy as it leaves your skin.
> A full heating curve, then, has flats and slopes. Slopes warm it. Flats rebuild it.
> Computing the heat absorbed during melting and boiling from the mass and the latent heat — that's yours.

## [26] قوانين الديناميكا الحرارية والإنتروبي
_The Laws of Thermodynamics & Entropy_
الفصل 6 · الدرس 6-2 · slug: `thermolaws` · الحالة: to-build

نص الحدس (كما يُروى في الفيديو):

> Two rules govern every engine, every fridge, every living thing. They sound almost too simple.
> First rule. Energy is never created and never destroyed. It only changes form, or moves.
> Heat into an engine becomes work out, plus heat dumped away. Add it all back: nothing missing. Ever.
> So the first rule says you can't win — you can't get more energy out than you put in.
> Then the second rule, the strange one. It's about direction.
> Drop ink in water; it spreads. You will never, ever watch it gather itself back into a drop.
> A hot coffee always cools to the room. The room never spontaneously reheats the coffee.
> Nothing in the first rule forbids the reverse. Energy would still balance. Yet it never happens.
> Because the universe drifts, always, toward more spread-out, more disordered, more mixed. We measure that drift with entropy.
> The second rule: total entropy always increases. That's why time has a direction.
> It's why no engine is perfect. Some energy must always be paid out as disordered waste heat. You can't even break even.
> Computing an engine's maximum possible efficiency from its hot and cold operating temperatures — that's yours.
